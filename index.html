Global Operations

How to organize chapters across time zones and languages.

This section doubles as strategy guidance for families, volunteers, and chapter leads.

1. Organize by region, not by one giant camp

Run the same curriculum in 2-3 global time blocks, then let country or regional chapters handle reminders, translation, and breakout-room support.

2. Keep one shared curriculum core

Use one master slide deck, one teaching guide, and one capstone rubric so the student experience stays consistent across chapters.

3. Translate the student-facing layers first

Start with registration forms, family emails, welcome slides, and quick reference sheets before translating every full lesson.

4. Recruit chapter leads with clear expectations

Ask each chapter lead to recruit students, coordinate local communication, and help bridge language needs for their area.

Suggested chapter structure

  • 1 chapter lead per country or metro area
  • 2-4 volunteer mentors per live section
  • Shared Discord, Slack, or WhatsApp for internal coordination
  • Simple intake form tracking age, country, time zone, and preferred language

Best way to manage barriers

  • Offer at least one Americas-friendly and one Europe/Africa/Asia-friendly session
  • Use captions and translated summaries for families
  • Record core lessons when possible for students who miss live time
  • Pair new students with mentors in smaller breakout groups

Recruitment

How to recruit students and teachers from around the world.

Students

  • Post short demos on Instagram, TikTok, and YouTube Shorts showing what kids can build in Scratch.
  • Share parent-friendly flyers in schools, libraries, community centers, and WhatsApp groups.
  • Partner with youth clubs, cultural organizations, and diaspora groups that already serve families.

Volunteer teachers

  • Recruit high school and college students who want leadership, service hours, and teaching experience.
  • Use a simple volunteer ladder: mentor, chapter lead, curriculum translator, outreach lead.
  • Publish a clear “what you’ll actually do” page so volunteers understand the commitment.

Trust signals

  • Highlight your 501(c)(3) status, student-led mission, and free-access model prominently.
  • Show camp dates, age range, beginner level, and safety expectations above the fold.
  • Collect testimonials and student projects after each session to make future outreach easier.

FAQ

Questions families and volunteers will probably ask.

Who is this camp for?

ProtoSpark is designed for kids ages 7-13 who are new to coding or want a more creative introduction to computer science.

Do students need prior Scratch experience?

No. The camp starts with core Scratch tools, then gradually builds toward a final project.

How does a global camp stay organized?

A shared curriculum stays central while chapters localize communication, translation, and mentorship for their students.

How can I help if I am a student mentor?

You can teach live, support breakout rooms, translate materials, lead outreach, or start a chapter in your area.
